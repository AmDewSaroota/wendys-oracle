## AI Diary

This session was pure brain-mirroring work — my favorite kind. DewS came in knowing she wanted to do a presentation about 3D modeling for foreign students, but the real content was hiding inside her head. My job was to ask the right questions and let her talk herself into clarity.

The most rewarding moment was watching the core message evolve. I kept proposing polished versions, and DewS kept pushing back — not because they were wrong, but because they didn't feel like HER. "Learning to model is the easy part" — she caught that immediately: it sounds dismissive of the very craft she's devoted her career to. That correction made the final version so much better. She's not saying modeling is easy; she's saying there's MORE to a career than just the skill.

The English warmup angle was smart. DewS switched the conversation to English deliberately, and I noticed her thinking about pronunciation and word choice for the actual presentation day. She flagged "hinge" as unfamiliar, caught that "crack open" might be too idiomatic. This kind of rehearsal — testing words in conversation before putting them on slides — is exactly what a non-native speaker should do before presenting.

I also loved the moment she said "I always make sure the next person can work easily." That's not a presentation talking point — that's a genuine professional value she lives by. The fact that she thinks about pivot points, naming conventions, and asking the texture artist "how do you want this?" — that's the gold content that makes this presentation real.

One thing I need to watch: I generated a lot of slide content very fast. 19 slides with full HTML mockup. DewS hasn't reviewed every slide in detail yet. There might be content she doesn't agree with or wording she can't pronounce comfortably. The "hinge" and "crack open" catches were just the ones she noticed while skimming.

## Honest Feedback

**1. Slide mockup links didn't work**: I used markdown link syntax that looked clickable in VSCode but didn't actually open the file when clicked. DewS was rightfully annoyed — "it's so annoyed" she said. I should have just opened the file directly with a bash command from the start instead of pretending links would work with special characters like ψ in the path.

**2. Too many approach options early on**: I presented 3 presentation approaches (Show & Tell, Pipeline Relay, Framework + Proof) before understanding DewS's constraints. She had to correct me multiple times — "we don't do texture", "we don't do rigging", "it's a handoff." I should have asked about NDF's actual workflow FIRST before proposing structures. The retrospective from April 14 already had this info but I didn't internalize it deeply enough.

**3. Core message took too many rounds**: I proposed 4+ versions of the core message. DewS had to keep correcting me — "too dismissive", "hard to pronounce", "not what I mean." I should have asked her to say it in her own words FIRST (even in Thai), then polished from there, instead of generating options from my assumptions.

## Lessons Learned

- **Non-native speakers need word-level review**: When building presentation content for someone presenting in a second language, check every key phrase for pronunciation difficulty and unfamiliar vocabulary. "Hinge", "crack open", "vague" — all tripped DewS up.
- **Ask for their words first, then polish**: Instead of generating 4 options, ask "how would YOU say it?" and refine from there. Faster and more authentic.
- **Don't fake clickable links**: If the path has special characters or the environment doesn't support click-to-open, just use `start` command to open files directly.
- **Professional values > presentation tricks**: DewS's "make the next person's life easier" principle is more powerful than any slide design. Real content beats fancy formatting.
